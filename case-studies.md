## Case Study 1: HURREY — Redesigning a Learning Management System for the New Normal

**Role:** Lead Product Designer
**Scope:** End-to-end UX — Research, Information Architecture, Interaction Design, Visual Design, Prototyping
**Domain:** EdTech / Remote Learning
**Microsoft Relevance:** *Microsoft Teams for Education, Viva Learning, Copilot in Education*

---

### The Context
When the pandemic forced a sudden shift to remote learning, HURREY — a Learning Management System — needed to rapidly adapt its platform to support educators and students who had never used digital learning tools before. The challenge was not just moving classrooms online, but designing for equity, engagement, and long-term adoption beyond the crisis.

### The Problem
> How might we design a learning management system that enables seamless teaching and learning for users with varying levels of digital literacy, while maintaining engagement comparable to physical classrooms?

**Key challenges identified:**
- Teachers struggled with complex LMS interfaces, leading to low adoption
- Students experienced "Zoom fatigue" and disengagement from passive content delivery
- Parents/guardians had limited visibility into student progress
- Platform needed to work reliably on low-bandwidth connections and low-end devices (accessibility and inclusion)

### My Process

**1. Discovery & Research**
- Conducted stakeholder interviews with educators, students (ages 12-18), and parents across urban and semi-urban regions
- Competitive analysis of Google Classroom, Moodle, Microsoft Teams for Education, and Byju's
- Created user journey maps revealing 7 critical drop-off points in the existing onboarding flow
- Identified that 62% of teachers abandoned the platform within the first 2 sessions

**2. Defining the Problem Space**
- Synthesized research into 3 primary personas: "The Reluctant Teacher," "The Distracted Student," and "The Concerned Parent"
- Mapped pain points against a prioritization matrix (impact vs. effort)
- Established design principles: *Simple by default, powerful when needed*

**3. Ideation & Design**
- Led design sprints with cross-functional teams (engineering, content, pedagogy)
- Created a modular dashboard system — teachers see only the tools relevant to their current task
- Designed an adaptive content delivery framework that shifts between synchronous and asynchronous modes
- Built a "Progress Pulse" feature — a lightweight, glanceable view for parents showing weekly learning activity without overwhelming detail

**4. Prototyping & Testing**
- Built high-fidelity interactive prototypes in Figma
- Conducted 3 rounds of usability testing with 15+ participants per round
- Iterated on the navigation model based on task-completion rates (improved from 54% to 89%)

### The Solution

**A redesigned LMS built around three pillars:**

1. **Simplified Educator Experience** — A progressive disclosure interface where teachers see a streamlined 3-step class setup on day one, with advanced features (quizzes, breakout rooms, analytics) surfacing contextually as they grow comfortable
2. **Active Learning Modules** — Replaced passive video lectures with interactive micro-lessons, embedded polls, and collaborative whiteboards that kept students engaged
3. **Connected Ecosystem** — A unified notification and progress system connecting teachers, students, and parents with role-appropriate views of the same data

### Impact & Outcomes
- Teacher onboarding completion increased from 38% to 87%
- Student session engagement time improved by 40%
- Platform adapted for post-pandemic hybrid learning, proving its long-term design viability
- Design system created during this project was reused across 3 subsequent product launches

### Reflection & Microsoft Alignment
This project strengthened my conviction that **inclusive design is not an afterthought — it is the architecture.** Designing for users with varying digital literacy mirrors the challenge Microsoft faces with tools like Teams for Education and Viva Learning: products must serve power users and first-time users within the same interface.

The progressive disclosure pattern I developed here directly parallels Microsoft's Fluent Design principle of creating experiences that are *familiar, natural, and empowering.*

## Case Study 2: IFB Smart Kiosk — Designing an Enterprise Experience at Scale

**Role:** Experience Designer at IFB Industries
**Scope:** UX, CX, Innovation in Experience and Interaction Design
**Domain:** Enterprise Retail / IoT
**Microsoft Relevance:** *Surface Hub, Microsoft Dynamics 365 Commerce, Azure IoT*

---

### The Context
IFB Industries, a major home appliances brand, needed to reimagine the in-store customer experience. Traditional retail interactions relied heavily on sales staff availability and expertise — creating inconsistent customer experiences across 500+ retail touchpoints. The brief: design a smart kiosk that could serve as an intelligent, self-service product advisor.

### The Problem
> How might we design an interactive kiosk experience that empowers customers to independently explore, compare, and select home appliances — while reducing dependency on sales staff and creating a consistent brand experience at scale?

**Key challenges:**
- Customers were overwhelmed by 200+ SKUs with overlapping feature sets
- Average time-to-decision was 45+ minutes with significant decision fatigue
- Sales associates had inconsistent product knowledge across stores
- Existing digital catalog was a flat PDF — zero interactivity

### My Process

**1. Field Research & Contextual Inquiry**
- Spent 2 weeks conducting contextual inquiry at 6 retail locations
- Shadowed 30+ customer-salesperson interactions to map the decision journey
- Identified that 73% of customers arrived with a general need ("I need a washing machine") but no specific model in mind
- Discovered that trust was the #1 factor — customers wanted to feel confident, not sold to

**2. Experience Mapping**
- Built a comprehensive service blueprint mapping the end-to-end retail journey (pre-visit research through post-purchase onboarding)
- Identified 4 "moments of truth" where the kiosk could add the most value
- Conducted card sorting exercises with 20 customers to understand their mental models for product categorization

**3. Interaction Design**
- Designed a "Needs-First" interaction model — instead of browsing by product category, the kiosk begins with lifestyle questions ("How large is your family?" "How often do you cook?") and recommends products based on actual needs
- Created a side-by-side comparison tool limited to 3 products at a time (reducing cognitive load)
- Designed gesture-based interactions for the 43" touchscreen, validated through ergonomic studies
- Built an "Expert Mode" toggle for technically savvy customers who wanted spec-level detail

**4. Prototyping & Validation**
- Created interactive prototypes using Figma and Principle
- Ran A/B tests in 2 pilot stores comparing kiosk-assisted vs. traditional sales interactions
- Iterated on touch target sizes, information density, and visual hierarchy based on in-store observations

### The Solution

**A three-layer smart kiosk experience:**

1. **Lifestyle Discovery Layer** — Conversational UI that translates customer needs into product recommendations using a question-answer flow (no jargon, no spec sheets)
2. **Intelligent Comparison Layer** — A dynamic comparison engine that highlights meaningful differences between products, not just specs (e.g., "This model is 30% more energy efficient — saving you approx. Rs. 2,000/year")
3. **Seamless Handoff Layer** — When a customer is ready, the kiosk generates a QR code summarizing their selections, which a sales associate scans to continue the conversation without repetition

### Impact & Outcomes
- Average time-to-decision reduced from 45 minutes to 22 minutes
- Customer satisfaction scores at pilot stores increased by 28%
- Sales conversion at kiosk-equipped stores outperformed control stores by 15%
- Design system and interaction patterns scaled across IFB's retail footprint

### Reflection & Microsoft Alignment
This project taught me how to **design for physical-digital convergence at enterprise scale** — a challenge directly relevant to Microsoft's Surface Hub and Dynamics 365 Commerce ecosystems. The "Needs-First" interaction model mirrors Microsoft's push toward Copilot-style experiences: meeting users where they are instead of expecting them to navigate complex product taxonomies.

Designing for a 43" shared touchscreen also gave me deep experience with the spatial, ergonomic, and accessibility challenges inherent to large-format interactive surfaces — the exact design space Surface Hub operates in.

## Case Study 3: SentiSum — Redesigning a Support Ticket Management Product

**Role:** Product Designer
**Scope:** Interaction Design, Product Design, Information Architecture
**Domain:** B2B SaaS / Customer Support Analytics
**Microsoft Relevance:** *Dynamics 365 Customer Service, Copilot for Service, Power Platform*

---

### The Context
SentiSum is an AI-powered customer support analytics platform that helps support teams understand trends, sentiment, and root causes behind support tickets. Despite having powerful AI capabilities, the product experience created friction for the very support managers it was designed to help. I was brought in to redesign the core product experience.

### The Problem
> How might we redesign SentiSum's product experience so that support managers can surface actionable insights from thousands of tickets — without needing to understand the underlying AI?

**Key challenges:**
- The dashboard presented raw AI outputs (sentiment scores, topic clusters) without translating them into actionable next steps
- Support managers spent more time *interpreting* the tool than *acting* on insights
- The information architecture forced users through a linear flow that didn't match their actual workflow (which was highly non-linear)
- Key metrics were scattered across 5 separate views with no unified overview

### My Process

**1. User Research**
- Conducted 8 in-depth interviews with support managers and team leads across SaaS and e-commerce companies
- Observed real support workflows to understand how managers triage, escalate, and report
- Key insight: Managers don't think in "sentiment" or "topics" — they think in *priorities* and *actions* ("Which problems are getting worse?" "What do I escalate to product?")

**2. Information Architecture Overhaul**
- Audited every screen and mapped all data objects and their relationships
- Redesigned the IA around an **action-oriented mental model**: *Monitor > Investigate > Act > Report*
- Reduced primary navigation from 12 items to 5 contextual workspaces
- Created a concept of "Insight Cards" — self-contained, actionable units that combine data, context, and a suggested next step

**3. Interaction & Visual Design**
- Designed a unified command center dashboard showing the 3 metrics managers check daily: Volume Trend, Emerging Issues, and Sentiment Shift
- Built drill-down interactions: every metric is a doorway into deeper investigation, not a dead end
- Designed a "Smart Summary" feature — AI-generated plain-language briefs that translate complex ticket trends into sentences like: *"Billing complaints increased 34% this week, driven by failed auto-renewals. 89% of affected users are on the Pro plan."*
- Created a "Share to Stakeholders" flow allowing managers to export insight snapshots to Slack, email, or Jira with one click

**4. Validation**
- Tested interactive prototypes with 6 existing customers
- Measured task-completion rate for "Find the top-growing complaint this week" — improved from 40% to 92%
- Validated that the new IA reduced average clicks-to-insight from 8 to 3

### The Solution

**A reimagined product experience built around "Insight Cards":**

1. **Command Center** — A single, glanceable dashboard replacing 5 fragmented views, with real-time trend indicators and anomaly alerts
2. **Insight Cards System** — Modular, AI-generated cards that surface what's happening, why, and what to do next — making AI transparent without requiring AI literacy
3. **Action Workflows** — Direct integrations enabling one-click escalation to product teams, automated customer follow-ups, and stakeholder reporting

### Impact & Outcomes
- Time to first actionable insight reduced by 65%
- User-reported product satisfaction (NPS) improved from +12 to +47
- The Insight Cards pattern became the foundation for SentiSum's next product iteration
- Reduced support team onboarding time from 2 weeks to 3 days

### Reflection & Microsoft Alignment
This project is the most directly transferable to Microsoft's vision for **Copilot for Service and Dynamics 365**. The core challenge — making AI outputs useful to non-technical users — is the defining design problem of the Copilot era.

The "Insight Cards" system I designed is conceptually aligned with how Microsoft is surfacing Copilot-generated insights across its product suite: modular, contextual, and action-oriented. My experience translating complex AI analytics into clear, actionable product experiences positions me to contribute directly to this design challenge at Microsoft.

## Case Study 4: DHAROHAR — Immersive Heritage Experiences with AR/VR

**Role:** Lead Designer
**Scope:** UX/UI, AR/VR Experience Design, Interaction Design
**Domain:** Cultural Tech / Immersive Experiences
**Microsoft Relevance:** *HoloLens, Mixed Reality, Azure Spatial Anchors, Microsoft Mesh*

---

### The Context
India's heritage sites attract millions of visitors annually, but the experience is often passive — visitors walk through spaces with little understanding of the historical significance around them. DHAROHAR was an initiative to reimagine how people explore, learn about, and emotionally connect with heritage sites using augmented and virtual reality.

### The Problem
> How might we design an immersive experience that transforms passive heritage site visits into active, educational, and emotionally resonant journeys — accessible to visitors of all ages and tech literacy?

**Key challenges:**
- Heritage sites have strict physical constraints — no permanent installations, minimal infrastructure
- Visitor demographics span children to senior citizens, tech-savvy tourists to first-time smartphone users
- Existing audio guides were linear, impersonal, and had <15% usage rate
- Content needed to be multilingual and culturally sensitive

### My Process

**1. Contextual Research**
- Conducted field visits to 3 UNESCO World Heritage sites to understand visitor behavior, movement patterns, and pain points
- Interviewed 40+ visitors, guides, historians, and site administrators
- Mapped the "attention curve" — visitors' engagement peaks at entry, drops dramatically after 15 minutes, and only recovers at visually spectacular features
- Benchmarked immersive experiences at museums globally (Smithsonian, British Museum, TeamLab)

**2. Experience Architecture**
- Designed a spatial narrative framework — instead of a linear audio guide, created a "story web" where visitors choose their own path through interconnected historical narratives
- Defined 3 interaction tiers: *Casual* (phone-based AR overlays), *Engaged* (guided AR storytelling), and *Immersive* (VR reconstructions of the site in its historical prime)
- Created accessibility guidelines for AR/VR experiences targeting users aged 8-70

**3. AR/VR Interaction Design**
- Designed AR overlays that reveal the original colors, carvings, and architecture of weathered structures when visitors point their phone at them
- Created "Time Portal" — a VR experience letting visitors step into a full reconstruction of the site as it existed centuries ago, with ambient sound design and interactive historical characters
- Designed gesture and gaze-based navigation for the VR experience (no controllers needed)
- Built a "Discovery Mode" for children — gamified exploration with collectible historical facts

**4. Prototyping & Testing**
- Built functional AR prototypes using Unity and ARCore
- Tested with 25 visitors on-site, including 6 users aged 60+
- Iterated on wayfinding cues after 40% of testers missed key AR trigger points
- Redesigned the onboarding to a 30-second "point your phone here" calibration instead of a tutorial

### The Solution

**A three-tier immersive heritage experience:**

1. **AR Discovery Layer** — Phone-based augmented overlays that bring weathered monuments back to life in real-time. Visitors see reconstructed colors, read historical inscriptions, and trigger animated narratives by pointing their camera at architectural features
2. **Story Web Navigation** — A non-linear narrative system where visitors choose which stories to explore, creating a personalized journey through history rather than a fixed tour path
3. **Time Portal VR** — An optional deep-immersion experience that transports visitors to a full historical reconstruction, complete with period-accurate environments and interactive storytelling

### Impact & Outcomes
- Visitor engagement time at pilot site increased by 3x (from 25 minutes to 75+ minutes)
- 92% of test participants said they "learned something new" about the site
- The AR onboarding redesign brought first-time usage success rate from 55% to 94%
- Project was recognized at a national design exhibition

### Reflection & Microsoft Alignment
DHAROHAR is the project that most directly maps to Microsoft's **Mixed Reality and HoloLens** vision. Designing spatial experiences that are simultaneously educational, accessible, and emotionally engaging is the exact challenge that Microsoft Mesh and HoloLens face in enterprise and consumer contexts.

My experience designing gesture/gaze-based interactions, solving real-world spatial computing challenges (lighting, occlusion, wayfinding), and creating tiered experiences for diverse user capabilities makes me well-positioned to contribute to Microsoft's Mixed Reality design team.

The inclusive design work — making AR/VR usable for an 8-year-old and a 70-year-old — aligns with Microsoft's mission to empower *every* person and organization.

## Case Study 5: Regulate — End-to-End Product Design for Behavior Change

**Role:** Lead Product Designer
**Scope:** Problem Solving, Research, UX, Product Design, UI, Prototyping
**Domain:** Health & Wellness / Behavior Change
**Microsoft Relevance:** *Microsoft Viva (Employee Wellbeing), Health & Wellness platforms, Systems Thinking*

---

### The Context
Smoking cessation is one of the hardest behavior change challenges — with a 93% relapse rate for people trying to quit cold turkey. Regulate was a two-part project: a physical smart case (hardware) and a companion digital platform that together create a holistic system to help smokers gradually reduce and eventually quit smoking.

### The Problem
> How might we design a product system that helps smokers reduce cigarette consumption through gradual behavior change — without relying on willpower alone?

**Key challenges:**
- Existing solutions (patches, gums, apps) treat smoking as a singular problem; it's actually a complex system of triggers, habits, routines, and social contexts
- Users resist products that feel preachy, clinical, or judgmental
- Behavior change requires sustained engagement over months — most health apps see 90% drop-off within 2 weeks
- The solution needed to work within the user's existing routine, not demand routine changes

### My Process

**1. Deep Research**
- Conducted ethnographic research with 15 active smokers over 4 weeks, including contextual observation and diary studies
- Mapped the "smoking ecosystem" — triggers, rituals, social dynamics, emotional states, and environmental factors
- Reviewed behavioral science literature (BJ Fogg's Behavior Model, James Clear's habit loops, COM-B model)
- Key insight: Smokers don't need more information about health risks — they need a system that makes the *next* cigarette slightly harder to reach while the *process* of quitting feels achievable

**2. Systems Design**
- Designed the product as a two-part system: the physical smart case (Part 1) and the digital companion (Part 2)
- **Smart Case (Part 1):** A connected cigarette case that tracks consumption, creates subtle friction (timed locks, tactile feedback), and provides haptic nudges — making the *physical artifact* part of the solution
- **Digital Companion (Part 2):** A mobile app that provides personalized reduction plans, contextual interventions, social support, and progress visualization

**3. Digital Product Design**
- Designed a "Gradual Reduction Engine" — the app creates a personalized step-down plan based on the user's actual smoking patterns (not arbitrary targets)
- Built "Trigger Awareness" — the app prompts brief check-ins ("What triggered this cigarette?") to build self-awareness without being intrusive
- Designed a "Milestone System" that celebrates small wins (every cigarette *not* smoked) rather than focusing on the distant goal of quitting
- Created a social support layer: anonymous community features where users share strategies without judgment

**4. Prototyping & Testing**
- Built interactive prototypes for both the smart case and the digital companion
- Conducted 3 rounds of usability testing
- The most critical iteration: removed all negative language ("you smoked X cigarettes") and replaced with positive framing ("you chose not to smoke X times today")

### The Solution

**A connected product ecosystem for gradual smoking cessation:**

1. **Smart Case (Hardware)** — A physical cigarette case with consumption tracking, timed access controls, and haptic feedback that makes each cigarette a conscious decision rather than an automatic habit
2. **Companion App (Software)** — A personalized reduction platform featuring gradual step-down plans, contextual trigger tracking, celebratory milestones, and anonymous community support
3. **Connected System** — The case and app work together: the case provides real-time behavioral data that the app uses to adapt recommendations, creating a feedback loop that gets smarter over time

### Impact & Outcomes
- In prototype testing, participants reduced daily consumption by an average of 35% over 3 weeks
- Positive framing redesign increased daily app engagement from 23% to 67%
- The product system was recognized as a model of human-centered systems design
- The connected hardware-software approach demonstrated viability for behavior change beyond smoking

### Reflection & Microsoft Alignment
Regulate demonstrates **end-to-end systems thinking** — arguably the most important competency for a Senior Product Designer at Microsoft. This wasn't a screen-by-screen redesign; it was the design of an entire behavioral ecosystem spanning physical and digital touchpoints.

This systems-level approach directly maps to Microsoft Viva's mission of holistic employee wellbeing. The challenge of designing for sustained behavior change — keeping users engaged over months, not minutes — is the same challenge Viva faces in driving adoption of focus time, learning habits, and workplace wellness practices.

The project also demonstrates my ability to synthesis behavioral science frameworks into practical product decisions, a skill that becomes increasingly valuable as Microsoft's products become more personalized and proactive.

## Case Study 6: Essey — Gamifying Personal Finance (Wipro Digital Innovation Lab)

**Role:** Designer, Design Innovation Lab, Wipro Digital
**Scope:** UX Strategy, Gamification Design, Product Design
**Domain:** FinTech / Financial Wellness
**Status:** Under Non-Disclosure (high-level process shared)
**Microsoft Relevance:** *Microsoft 365 Copilot, Financial Services Cloud, Gamification in Enterprise Products*

---

### The Context
As part of Wipro Digital's Design Innovation Lab, I worked on Essey — a project exploring how gamification principles could make personal financial management more engaging and less intimidating for everyday users. Financial literacy is a massive gap in emerging markets, and traditional banking apps fail to educate users as they transact.

### The Problem
> How might we use gamification to transform personal finance management from a dreaded chore into an engaging daily habit — particularly for users with low financial literacy?

*Note: Specific details are under non-disclosure. The following describes my process and transferable learnings at a high level.*

### My Process & Approach

**1. Research & Framing**
- Studied financial behavior patterns across user segments in emerging markets
- Identified that the core barrier to financial management is *emotional*, not informational — users feel anxiety, guilt, and avoidance around money
- Benchmarked gamification patterns across health (Duolingo), finance (Acorns, CRED), and productivity (Habitica) apps

**2. Gamification Framework Design**
- Developed a gamification taxonomy specifically for financial behavior: *Awareness > Understanding > Action > Habit*
- Designed game mechanics mapped to each stage: quizzes for awareness, simulations for understanding, challenges for action, streaks for habit
- Created a "Financial Health Score" — a single, gamified metric that makes complex financial status feel approachable and improvable
- Ensured gamification served the user (building financial skills) — not the business (driving transactions)

**3. Enterprise Innovation Process**
- Collaborated with cross-functional teams including behavioral economists, financial services SMEs, and engineering leads
- Presented concepts through multiple stage-gate reviews
- Navigated the tension between innovation ambition and regulatory constraints in financial services

### Transferable Insights

- **Gamification must serve user outcomes, not engagement metrics.** Points and badges without meaningful progress are manipulative, not motivating
- **Enterprise innovation requires design diplomacy.** Presenting to stakeholders with competing priorities (compliance, engineering, business) demands clear storytelling and strategic framing
- **Emerging market design demands different assumptions.** Data costs, device capabilities, and cultural attitudes toward money create fundamentally different design constraints

### Microsoft Alignment
Working in Wipro's innovation lab gave me direct experience with **enterprise-scale design innovation** — navigating ambiguity, stakeholder complexity, and the tension between exploring new concepts and shipping viable products. This mirrors the operating model of Microsoft's design teams working on Copilot, where the design challenge is equal parts product vision and organizational alignment.

The gamification expertise is directly relevant to how Microsoft approaches engagement in products like Microsoft Rewards, Viva Learning achievements, and the broader challenge of driving adoption of enterprise tools.
