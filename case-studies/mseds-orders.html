Programme Definition

What I built & why it mattered

I was the design owner for Customer Self-Serve Orders — a multi-phase programme to transform enterprise ordering from manual, operations-dependent workflows into a self-serve, scalable experience. I anchored every design decision on the Plan of Record (POR), which established the north star: customer autonomy paired with operational safety.

My first framing decision was critical: I treated self-serve as a trust problem, not a speed problem. Enterprise customers don’t just want faster checkout — they need confidence that pricing is enrolment-accurate, eligibility rules are enforced, and compliance paths can’t be broken. This framing set the direction for the entire programme.

Plan of Record (POR)
  • I established the north star for self-serve (customer autonomy + ops safety)
  • Defined in-scope / out-of-scope guardrails critical to UX decisions
  • Anchored design on SKU-level pricing visibility and auditability
Scope of Work
  • Translated contractual and pricing constraints into usable flows
  • Designed for true-ups, step-ups, new orders, reservation reconciliation
  • Ensured role-based access and action visibility on MAC
Design Impact

My framing decision — treating self-serve as a trust problem rather than a speed problem — set the direction for the entire programme. Every downstream design choice (upfront eligibility, quote previews, audit trails) flowed from this reframe.

AI in my process

I used Copilot to rapidly synthesise the POR’s contractual and pricing constraints into structured design principles, compressing days of analysis into hours and letting me focus on framing rather than extraction.

My Role & Ownership

Design owner for Orders

Over multiple semesters, I evolved into the design owner for Orders, responsible for strategy, end-to-end flow design, partner validation (cogwalks), design-to-dev execution, accessibility readiness (Grade C met), bug-bash and fit-and-finish ownership, and launch success.

Design Impact

Research & Validation

I reframed the problem: trust, not speed

I led product discovery and synthesised customer pain points into design principles. The critical insight: enterprise customers didn’t need faster checkout. They needed confidence that their orders were correct, compliant, and auditable. I reframed “self-serve” from a speed play to a trust play — and this shift shaped everything we built.

Partner Cogwalks & Feedback

I led and participated in multiple partner and ROC/OSC cognitive walkthroughs to validate delighters, pain points, and usability gaps. These weren’t usability tests — they were confidence tests. I was looking for where partners hesitated, where they second-guessed the system, and where they needed proactive guidance.

“My head is spinning. This split works just grand, it’s awesome.” — Partner, Cogwalk feedback

“This will make our lives super easy.” — Partner, Cogwalk feedback

  • Identified need for proactive guidance + “flag for review” patterns
  • Informed bulk actions, pricing visibility, and audit trails
  • Applied behavioural and value lens: what “self-serve” actually means for enterprise is outcome confidence
Design Impact

The trust reframe wasn’t obvious. Other teams were optimising for speed and task completion. I chose confidence as the primary UX KPI — and the adoption numbers validated that choice.

AI in my process

I used AI tools to synthesise cogwalk transcripts into structured insight clusters — compressing 2–3 days of affinity mapping into a focused half-day. This let me iterate on design principles faster and share findings with PM within the same sprint.

Design Execution

What I designed — and the impact of each decision

UX & Content Validation

I caught and removed ambiguous terms (“contract” → “enrolment”) that were causing downstream confusion. I ensured every message matched legal, compliance, and customer mental models — directly reducing downstream clarification tickets and rework.

Specs & North Star

I partnered with PM to shape behavioural scenarios and ensured design feasibility under RP, pricing, and compliance constraints. My UX risk signals directly influenced fast-follow prioritisation — preventing scope creep from undermining launch quality.

Design Walkthroughs (Owned)

I used walkthroughs to align PM, Eng, Ops, and Leadership — surfacing UX risks early (validation gaps, cognitive load, flow breaks). These sessions reduced late-stage surprises before UAT/GA and built cross-team confidence in the design.

E2E Flow Ownership

I owned the full flow: eligibility → pricing → quote → PO → submission → management. Multi-PO, bulk editing, multi-SKU search. I treated quote previews as high-trust artefacts, not intermediate steps — a choice that proved critical for adoption.

Design Impact

My decision to surface eligibility and pricing upfront — before the customer committed to an order — is the single design choice most responsible for the ~60% time reduction. It prevented errors instead of recovering from them.

AI in my process

I used AI to rapidly generate and evaluate UX copy variants, test message clarity against compliance requirements, and draft spec language — cutting content-validation cycles from days to hours.

AI-Accelerated Design

How AI made me faster without replacing my judgement

This project was one of the places where we worked with AI to make the process faster. After permutations and combinations and quick experimentation, we were able to take the project to success. AI didn’t design the experience — I did. But AI compressed the time between raw input and structured thinking, letting me spend more time on the decisions that mattered.

Research Synthesis

Used AI to cluster cogwalk transcripts and feedback into insight themes. What took 2–3 days of affinity mapping became a focused half-day. I still made every framing call — but AI surfaced the patterns faster.

Scenario Mapping

Used Copilot to rapidly generate permutations of order scenarios (true-ups, step-ups, bulk, mixed-SKU). This let me stress-test flows against edge cases before dev handoff, catching issues that would have surfaced in UAT.

Content Validation

Used AI to generate and evaluate UX copy variants against compliance requirements. Cut content-validation cycles from days to hours — critical when legal language had to be precise but human-readable.

Spec & Handover Drafting

Used AI to draft spec structures, handover checklists, and scenario documentation. ~30% reduction in repetitive content and handover effort — freeing time for design-to-dev collaboration.

Design Impact

AI compressed the time between raw input and structured thinking. I still made every framing call. But AI let me make those calls faster, iterate more, and ship with higher confidence. The ~30% reduction in handover effort meant I could spend that time on what actually moves outcomes: partner validation and cross-team alignment.

Validation & UAT

Ensuring trust before launch

I ran design-focused bug-bashes and UAT walkthroughs, treating every issue through a trust lens. This wasn’t just quality assurance — it was about ensuring no trust-breaking issue shipped to customers.

  • I blocked three issues engineering had marked as low-priority but that would have undermined quote confidence at GA
  • Logged UX-critical issues (validation, messaging, affordances) and tracked them to resolution
  • Used UAT feedback (pricing, currency, eligibility) as design signals, not just bugs — feeding insights back into fast-follow prioritisation
  • Ensured Orders + Credits GA delivered with Accessibility Grade-C compliance met
Design Impact

My fit-and-finish ownership became a turning point. I turned a neglected phase into a design-quality gate — catching trust-breaking issues before they reached customers.
