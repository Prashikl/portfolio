Design that walks off the screen, and into the room.

A practice runs deeper when its designers care about more than their pixels. Hackathon T-shirts, Pinnacle editions, and Studio artefacts — each one a design object with a specific job: translate ideas into wearable form, reinforce a shared identity, and let the culture remember itself.

i
The premise

The studio is the product, too

A design studio shapes the products that ship. It also — quietly — shapes the people who ship them. The artefacts a studio puts in its people's hands are part of that shaping. T-shirts, posters, stickers, editioned prints. Treated casually, they're swag. Treated like design, they're culture infrastructure.

I made a small practice of treating studio artefacts as design problems. Each one had a brief, a constraint, a reason to exist. None of them were "we should do a tee."

ii
The work

Three artefacts, three jobs

01

Hackathon T-shirts

Translated AI and experimentation themes into wearable form. The brief was tight — every shirt had to make sense to a stranger in an elevator, and to a teammate three years later. The motifs were typographic, restrained, slightly weird in the way that survives a second look.

02

Pinnacle editions

A limited-run editioned piece for the studio's annual recognition moment. Numbered. Hand-finished. Designed to feel like an object somebody would keep on a desk for a decade — not a souvenir from a one-night party.

03

Studio artefacts

Posters, stickers, and small printed objects produced for studio rituals — kickoffs, retros, handovers. Each one a punctuation mark in the studio's calendar. Together, they form an identity the studio can recognise itself by.

Working principle

A T-shirt is a brief. A poster is a brief. An editioned print is a brief. Treat them like one, and the culture changes shape.

iii
The philosophy

Why physical, when everything else is screen

Software ships and disappears. The work is real, but it lives behind a login. Physical artefacts are witnessable. They sit on a desk. They get worn to a coffee shop. They get noticed by somebody who didn't ask to be marketed to.

For a studio whose work is mostly invisible to the outside world, the physical artefact is one of the few places the practice can be seen and recognised. That's worth investing real design energy in.

a.

Identity, made wearable

A studio that wears its own work — confidently, not corporately — broadcasts confidence. People notice a designer wearing a thoughtful tee. They don't notice a designer wearing a sponsor logo.

b.

Memory, made object

Studios forget. People rotate. Projects ship and recede. An editioned object — dated, numbered, well-made — is how a studio remembers what it did, in a form that survives reorgs.

c.

Culture, made tangible

The objects say what the studio takes seriously. Everything else is a slogan.

iv
Why it matters

The practice is wider than the product

The work that ships is the visible practice. The work that doesn't ship — the artefacts, the rituals, the small careful objects — is where a studio's character shows. Both matter. The first earns the salary. The second earns the loyalty.

Lesson

A designer who only designs what ships is doing half the job. The other half is the culture that ships them.
