Leading without authority, when the clock is the only rule.

Hackathons are tiny operating environments where the org's defaults stop applying — and where leadership reveals itself fast. Across multiple Microsoft hackathons, I led design on AI-native prototypes, reframed ideas toward revenue enablement, and pulled cross-functional teams toward shippable thinking inside 72-hour windows.

i
The summary

What hackathons reveal

A hackathon is a compressed org. Strangers, no roadmap, no authority gradient, an ambiguous brief, a hard deadline. The same patterns that make a designer effective in the org show up — sharpened, undisguised — in the hackathon.

Executive summary

In 72 hours, a team either finds the bet that's worth taking — or burns the clock on the bet that came in the room. The design lead's job is to make sure it's the first one.

ii
Core contributions

Four moves I made, every hackathon

01

Reframed ideas toward revenue enablement

Most hackathon ideas arrive in the room as features. I'd push the team to restate them as revenue or operating-cost levers — what does this unlock, for whom, and how would the business measure it? Half the ideas died at this step. The survivors were sharper.

02

Built AI-native prototypes under tight timelines

Wired the model into the prototype on day one — not as a final-day flourish. The design moves with the model: prompts, fallback states, hallucination handling, refusal flows. The model is a first-class material, not a feature toggle.

03

Aligned cross-functional teams without authority

Engineers, PMs, researchers, sometimes field — none of them reported to me. I led by writing the brief on the wall, returning to it whenever the conversation drifted, and making sure every decision linked back to the line at the top. Influence is mostly housekeeping.

04

Owned the demo

A hackathon ends in a demo, and a demo is its own design problem. I scripted, rehearsed, and edited every team demo I led — to land the insight, not the screens. Multiple of those demos went on to become real bets in the org.

iii
What hackathons taught me

Lessons that travel back into the day job

a.

Frame fast, refine slow

The first six hours are framing. Get the framing wrong and no amount of polish in the last six hours rescues it. The same is true of quarter-long projects.

b.

A prototype is a hypothesis with a UI

The fidelity is whatever serves the hypothesis. Pretty without point is wasted. Ugly with point is gold.

c.

The team is the design

The same idea, with two different teams, becomes two different products. Designing the team's working agreement is part of designing the output.

d.

Demos are decisions

A demo isn't a presentation; it's the moment a bet either becomes real or doesn't. Treat it as a designed object — beats, framing, payoff, ask.

iv
Why it matters

Hackathons are leadership in miniature

The org promotes for a reason that a hackathon strips bare: can you, given a vague brief and an unfamiliar team, produce a thing the business takes seriously? The answer is mostly about framing, focus, and finishing — and very little about the brief itself.

Lesson

Leadership is not a title issued by an org chart. It is the willingness to write the brief on the wall, and to keep returning the team to it.
