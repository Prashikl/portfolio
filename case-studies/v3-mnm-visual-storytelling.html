Translating effort into impact, in a language leadership reads.

Design work loses to better-told stories every day. MnM is a framework I authored to map critical journey moments to business signals and measurable outcomes — so the room sees not what we made, but what changed because we made it.

i
The problem

Good work, untold, is invisible work

In a leadership readout, you have ninety seconds before the room moves on. A wall of screenshots will not survive those ninety seconds. A list of features will not survive them either. What survived, in every readout I sat in, was a sentence about a metric that the executive could repeat to someone else.

So the design problem became: how do we ensure the work consistently produces those sentences — and how do we hand teams a framework so they can produce them without me in the room?

ii
The framework

Moments & Metrics, as a coupled pair

The framework is deliberately small. Two halves. Always together.

M1

Moments

The handful of journey points where the user's experience changes meaningfully. Not every screen — only the moments where trust is won, lost, or compressed. Usually three to seven per project.

M2

Metrics

For each moment, a paired business signal — adoption, deal velocity, error rate, ops cost, NPS. The metric is what the moment is for. If a moment doesn't move a metric, it isn't a moment; it's a screen.

Rule

A moment without a metric is decoration. A metric without a moment is a number nobody owns.

iii
The visual language

Two diagrams, one narrative

01

MnM timelines

A horizontal timeline of the user journey, with each Moment marked above the line and its paired Metric below. Reading top-to-bottom gives the experience; reading left-to-right gives the story; the eye does both at once.

02

Sankey-style effort-to-impact

A flow diagram showing design investment fanning out into business outcomes. The width of each band is proportional to the effort committed. Leaders see at a glance where the team spent, and what that spend produced.

03

The one-paragraph narrative

Below the diagrams sits a single paragraph: the story the executive can repeat. If the paragraph requires the diagrams to make sense, the diagrams aren't doing their job. If the diagrams require the paragraph to make sense, neither is.

04

The receipts appendix

Behind the one-pager, a deeper appendix. Same structure, fuller evidence. Read by anyone who wants to challenge the headline. The headline holds because the appendix is there.

iv
Outcomes

What changed when teams adopted MnM

Before

Readouts opened with screenshots. The room asked "and?" The team scrambled.

After

Readouts opened with a one-paragraph narrative tied to two metrics. The room asked "what's next?"

90s readouts
Before

Designers defended pixels. PMs defended scope. Both lost.

After

Both defended a shared set of moments and metrics. Conflict moved upstream — to the framing, where it belongs.

Aligned framings
Before

"What did design contribute?" was answered with adjectives.

After

It was answered with a sentence about a metric, repeated up the chain.

Repeatable narrative
v
Why it matters

Communication is part of the design

The framework is small on purpose. A communication tool that requires a forty-page playbook to use is not a communication tool — it is a second job. MnM is intentionally something a senior designer can absorb in fifteen minutes and apply to their next readout.

Lesson

Design that does not survive its readout did not happen, as far as the business is concerned. The readout is part of the work.
