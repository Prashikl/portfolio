Setting the Stage

Enterprise ordering is not checkout UX

Before I could design anything, I needed to understand what ordering actually means in enterprise commerce. This isn’t an e-commerce cart. It’s a high-stakes system where pricing must be enrolment-accurate, eligibility rules must be enforced, compliance paths can’t be broken, and a single mistake creates downstream instability across billing, provisioning, and audit.

The existing workflow was manual and fragmented. Partners called operations teams. Operations teams keyed orders into legacy tools. Customers had zero visibility into what was happening. The organisation needed a self-serve path — but one that preserved every constraint the manual process enforced.

Before: Manual & operations-dependent
  • Partners relied on phone/email to place orders
  • Operations teams manually entered data into POET/VLCT
  • No pricing visibility until after order submission
  • Average order cycle measured in days, not minutes
After: Self-serve with trust built in
  • Partners and customers create orders independently
  • Upfront eligibility checks and real-time pricing
  • Quote previews as auditable, high-trust artefacts
  • Order creation collapsed to minutes

The Design Process

Six phases from ambiguity to launch

This wasn’t a linear double diamond. Enterprise design at this scale requires constant negotiation between what’s ideal, what’s feasible, and what’s safe. Here’s how I structured the work.

1

I sat with ops teams before I opened Figma

Weeks 1–3

I started by mapping the existing operational workflow end-to-end. Not from documentation — from sitting with operations teams, watching them key orders into POET, and asking “why do you do it this way?” at every step. I needed to understand the invisible constraints before I could design around them.

I synthesised the Plan of Record’s contractual and pricing constraints into structured design principles. This turned a 60+ page programme document into actionable guardrails I could reference throughout the project.

  • Mapped current-state workflows across Partner, OSC/ROC, and Customer personas
  • Identified 12 distinct order types with unique compliance requirements
  • Documented pricing rules, eligibility logic, and audit requirements
  • Interviewed operations staff to surface tacit knowledge not in any spec

2

The brief said speed. The real problem was trust.

Weeks 3–5

This is where the most important design decision happened. The brief said “make ordering faster.” But after discovery, I realised speed wasn’t the real problem. Trust was.

Enterprise customers didn’t need faster checkout. They needed confidence that their orders were correct, compliant, and auditable. Partners were slow not because the UI was bad, but because they couldn’t verify pricing, eligibility, or compliance without calling someone.

I reframed the problem statement from “How do we speed up ordering?” to “How do we give users enough confidence to act independently?”

Key Reframe

Self-serve in enterprise is not a speed problem. It’s a trust problem. Users won’t self-serve if they can’t verify outcomes before committing. This single insight shaped every downstream decision.

Design Principles I Established
  • Trust before speed — Surface constraints upfront, not after errors
  • Prevent, don’t recover — Eligibility checks before commitment
  • Transparency as UX — Pricing and compliance always visible
  • Confidence to act — Every screen answers “Am I doing this right?”
Personas Prioritised
  • Partners — Most frequent users, time-pressured, need bulk efficiency
  • OSC/ROC Ops — Complex scenarios, compliance-critical
  • Enterprise Customers — Low frequency, high stakes, need guided confidence

3

I designed for constraint visibility, not feature richness

Weeks 5–10

I designed the full end-to-end flow: eligibility → pricing → quote → PO → submission → management. But the key decisions weren’t about layout or components. They were about where to surface information and where to enforce constraints.

Information Architecture Decisions

I structured the ordering flow around progressive disclosure of constraints. Each step reveals only what’s needed to make the next decision confidently:

  1. Entry point — Clear paths: “Create order” and “View orders” replaced fragmented navigation
  2. Contract selection — Compliance status, anniversary dates, and coverage windows visible before committing
  3. Product catalogue — Inline editing with real-time subtotals, multi-SKU search, and bulk selection
  4. Cart & review — Full pricing transparency, quote downloads as auditable artefacts, split-order tools
  5. Order management — Proactive status, draft management, auto-deletion notices
Critical Design Decision

I chose to surface eligibility and pricing upfront, before order commitment — not as validation after submission. This meant more engineering complexity on the front end. But it prevented errors instead of recovering from them. This single decision became the primary driver of the ~60% time reduction.

Interaction Design Details

  • Multi-PO support — Submit up to 50 orders at once, reducing repetitive workflows
  • Bulk editing — Table-level edits for quantity and pricing across multiple SKUs
  • Save as draft — A trust pattern: users can pause without losing work
  • Quote preview downloads — Treated as high-trust artefacts, not intermediate steps
  • Proactive guidance — Contextual messages replacing ambiguous terms (“contract” → “enrolment”)

4

I tested confidence, not just task completion

Weeks 8–14 (overlapping with design)

I led and participated in multiple rounds of cognitive walkthroughs with partners and ROC/OSC operations teams. These weren’t standard usability tests. I was testing for confidence, not just task completion.

How I Structured the Cogwalks

Setup & Recruitment

Recruited partners with varying order complexity (simple new orders, bulk step-ups, multi-PO scenarios). Mixed experience levels — power users alongside infrequent users. 6 partner sessions across 2 rounds, plus dedicated ROC/OSC walkthroughs.

Protocol & What I Measured

Think-aloud protocol with scenario-based tasks. I measured not just task success, but hesitation points, confidence levels (self-reported), and moments of second-guessing. I wanted to know where users lost trust in what the system was showing them.

What the Cogwalks Revealed

Pain Point

Partners hesitated at contract selection — they couldn’t tell which enrolments were eligible without checking externally

Design Response

Surfaced compliance status, coverage windows, and anniversary dates directly in the selection table

Pain Point

Users wanted to verify pricing before committing but had no way to preview the full quote

Design Response

Introduced downloadable quote preview as an auditable artefact — this became the highest-trust feature in the flow

Delighter

The split-order tool and multi-PO submission were called out as “game changers” for high-volume partners

Validation

Confirmed bulk editing as a first-class flow, not a bolt-on — increased priority in the dev roadmap

Insight

The term “contract” caused confusion — partners used “enrolment” in their mental model

Design Response

Replaced all instances of “contract” with “enrolment” — reducing downstream clarification tickets

5

Four iterations the cogwalks forced

Weeks 12–18

I didn’t treat cogwalk findings as a backlog to triage. I used them to pressure-test every assumption in the design. Some findings confirmed my direction. Others forced significant rework. Here’s what changed.

Iteration 1: Contract Selection Rework

Partners couldn’t assess eligibility at a glance. I redesigned the selection table to surface compliance status, anniversary dates, and coverage windows inline — removing the need to cross-reference external tools.

Iteration 2: Cart as Trust Surface

The cart was initially a summary screen. After cogwalks, I redesigned it as a trust verification step — with separate sections for new products and reconciliations, full pricing transparency, and downloadable quotes.

Iteration 3: Content & Language Audit

Replaced “contract” with “enrolment” across the entire flow. Rewrote error messages to be prescriptive, not descriptive. Added contextual guidance where partners previously second-guessed the system.

Iteration 4: Proactive Order Management

Added draft auto-deletion notices, submitted-order filtering, and contract-level grouping. Designed the management view as a confidence surface, not just a list.

Before iteration
  • “Contract” terminology confused partners
  • Cart was a passive summary screen
  • No eligibility visibility until submission error
  • Error messages described problems, didn’t guide action
After iteration
  • “Enrolment” matched partner mental model
  • Cart redesigned as a trust verification surface
  • Compliance status visible inline at selection
  • Error messages prescriptive: told users what to do next

I chose to deprioritise advanced filtering in Phase 1 to invest in quote preview and pricing transparency. This was a deliberate trade-off: partners could manage without filters, but they couldn’t self-serve without pricing confidence. The data validated this — quote downloads became the highest-used feature within 30 days.

6

I turned bug-bash into a design-quality gate

Weeks 16–22

I owned fit-and-finish through bug-bash, UAT walkthroughs, and accessibility validation. This wasn’t a handoff phase — it was a design-quality gate.

Bug-Bash as Design Practice

I ran design-focused bug-bashes treating every issue through a trust lens. I blocked three issues that engineering had marked as low-priority but would have undermined quote confidence at GA. I logged UX-critical issues (validation gaps, messaging inconsistencies, affordance problems) and tracked each to resolution.

UAT as a Research Instrument

I used UAT feedback as design signals, not just defect reports. Pricing discrepancies, currency formatting issues, and eligibility edge cases all fed back into fast-follow prioritisation. This approach turned a compliance gate into a continuous learning loop.

Accessibility

Ensured Orders + Credits GA delivered with Accessibility Grade-C compliance met. Keyboard navigation, screen reader compatibility, and colour contrast validated across all order flows.

Post-Launch Monitoring

Tracked adoption velocity, completion rates, and support ticket patterns in the first 30 days. ~80% adoption within 30 days validated the trust-first approach — users self-served immediately, not gradually.

Navigating Stakeholders

Influence without authority

Senior design work isn’t just about making the right decisions — it’s about getting others to trust and act on them. Here are four moments where influence shaped outcomes.

Engineering Alignment
Upfront eligibility meant more front-end complexity

Engineering pushed back on surfacing compliance checks before order commitment — it added API calls and loading states. I didn’t just argue for better UX. I showed them the downstream cost: every post-submission error triggered a support ticket, an ops intervention, and a trust loss. The upfront check was cheaper than the recovery path. They agreed.

PM Negotiation
Requirements shifted mid-sprint. I used design principles as the anchor.

When PM wanted to add a feature that conflicted with the “prevent, don’t recover” principle, I didn’t block it — I reframed the ask through the lens of the principles we’d agreed on. We found a solution that met the business need without breaking the trust model. The principles became the neutral ground.

Cross-Team Alignment
PM, Eng, and Ops were drifting into internal priorities

I ran a 45-minute working session that got PM, Engineering, and Ops aligned on the next sprint’s scope. Every time the conversation drifted to internal priorities, I pulled it back to the user’s perspective — what the partner would experience, not what was easiest to build. We left with a shared decision, not a compromise.

Executive Narrative
Design needed to be visible at leadership level

I deliberately built the before/after narrative for LT and exec demos. I didn’t present self-serve as a UI improvement — I positioned it as a scale enabler. Reduced ops cost, faster deal cycles, legacy retirement. The acclaim from LT wasn’t accidental. It was a design decision about how to frame the work.

User Testing Summary

What testing taught me that the spec couldn’t

Across cogwalks, UAT, and post-launch analytics, three themes consistently emerged. These weren’t in the original requirements. They came from watching real users interact with the system.

Theme 1: Confidence outweighs convenience

Users consistently chose slower paths when they felt more confident about the outcome. The “review and download quote” step added time to the flow — but partners used it on nearly every order. Removing it would have been faster but less trusted.

Theme 2: Language shapes behaviour

Changing “contract” to “enrolment” didn’t just fix confusion — it changed how partners navigated the flow. Terminology that matched their mental model reduced hesitation at every step. Content design was as impactful as interaction design.

Theme 3: Error prevention builds more trust than error recovery

Surfacing eligibility upfront didn’t just prevent mistakes. It changed the user’s posture from cautious to confident. When users know they can’t make certain errors, they move faster and engage more deeply with the features that matter.

Reflections

What I’d do differently and what I’d keep

If I did this again

A moment I was wrong. My first cart design was a passive summary screen. I thought it was clean and efficient. Cogwalks proved otherwise — partners didn’t trust what they couldn’t verify. I had to rework the entire cart into a trust verification step with separate sections, downloadable quotes, and full pricing transparency. The rework was significant, but it became the highest-trust surface in the entire flow.

A bias I discovered. I was unconsciously designing for power users (high-volume partners) and nearly overlooked the infrequent enterprise customer who orders once a quarter. Cogwalk round 2 caught this — a first-time user couldn’t figure out contract selection because I’d assumed familiarity with enrolment structures. I added contextual guidance that helped both personas without slowing down power users.

What changed permanently. Bug-bash as design practice is now something I advocate for on every project. Before this project, fit-and-finish was treated as a QA phase. I turned it into a design-quality gate — catching three trust-breaking issues that engineering had marked as low-priority. This approach is now part of how I work, not just how I worked here.

A trade-off that still sits with me. I chose to deprioritise advanced filtering in Phase 1 to invest in quote preview and pricing transparency. The data validated the call — quote downloads became the most-used feature. But some partners felt the gap, and I heard it in round 2 cogwalks. I’d invest in a lightweight filter earlier in Phase 2 if I did it again. Not every right decision feels clean.
