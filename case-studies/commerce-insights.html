Commerce Insights — Decision System

I transformed an enterprise seller dashboard from a reporting surface into a trusted, action-driven decision system — enabling sellers to confidently decide what to do next, not just understand what data exists.

Context & Problem

Rich data, poor decisions

Commerce Insights is a seller-facing platform aggregating enterprise commerce data across agreements, renewals, subscriptions, concessions, L+SA, and lifecycle milestones. The platform had comprehensive data — but sellers couldn’t use it to make decisions.

What Sellers Had
  • Multiple data sources across renewals, subscriptions, and concessions
  • Dashboard views with comprehensive metrics
  • Export-to-Excel as the primary workflow
What Sellers Needed
  • Clarity on what needs action now
  • Confidence to act directly without offline analysis
  • Trust that the data was fresh, sourced, and reliable
Design Impact

The core issue was not data availability — it was the absence of judgement encoded into the experience. Data without direction is just noise. My role was to turn noise into signal.

My Role & Ownership

Design owner for Commerce Insights

I was the design owner responsible for the information architecture, interaction strategy, and visual system for Commerce Insights. My scope covered:

  • Reframing the product direction from reporting to decision-enablement
  • Workshop facilitation to align PM, Eng, and sellers on what “actionable” actually means
  • Widget-based dashboard architecture with guardrails for flexibility
  • Seller validation sessions to test concepts and refine priorities
  • Design system alignment to ensure scalability and consistency
Design Impact

I didn’t inherit a brief and execute — I shaped the product direction. The shift from reporting to decision-enablement was a design-led reframe that changed what this product could become.

Design Strategy

Three principles that changed the product

I reframed Commerce Insights around three principles that became the north star for all design decisions:

01
Decision over Reporting

Prioritised signals and next actions instead of exhaustive metrics. Every widget had to answer: “What should I do next?”

02
Trust as a UX Requirement

Surfaced data freshness, source context, and confidence cues so sellers could act without second-guessing.

03
Speed to Decision

Highlighted deltas, risk, and change — not raw values. Reduced time-to-decision by showing what changed and why it matters.

Design Impact

These principles weren’t abstract — they became acceptance criteria. Every widget, every layout decision, every data presentation choice was evaluated against them. They gave the team a shared language for “good enough to ship.”

Design Execution

What I designed — and the decisions behind it

Action-Framed Insights

I used the framing “As a seller, I can ___ so that ___” to define the value of every widget. This forced the conversation from “what data do we show?” to “what action does this enable?”

Widget-Based Architecture

I explored modular, widget-based dashboard models with guardrails for flexibility. Sellers could personalise views without creating chaos — structured freedom, not freeform.

Trust Signals

Every data surface included freshness indicators, source context, and confidence cues. Sellers needed to know when data was last updated and where it came from before they’d act on it.

Risk & Delta Highlighting

Instead of showing raw renewal values, I surfaced what changed, what’s at risk, and what needs attention. This reduced cognitive load and let sellers focus on exceptions, not totals.

AI in my process

I used AI tools to rapidly cluster and structure insight ideas from workshops, compressing ideation and affinity-mapping into focused sessions. This let me iterate on widget concepts faster and bring structured options to stakeholder reviews within the same sprint.

Research & Validation

Validated with real sellers, not assumptions

I validated concepts with real sellers through live feedback sessions. These weren’t just usability tests — they were trust tests. I was looking for where sellers hesitated, where they reached for Excel instead of acting in-tool, and where confidence broke down.

  • Ran structured feedback sessions with sellers across different segments
  • Identified trust gaps where data was present but sellers wouldn’t act on it
  • Mapped the “Excel escape hatch” pattern — every time a seller exported data, it indicated a missing decision cue in the interface
  • Iterated on widget prioritisation based on seller mental models, not system architecture
Design Impact

The “Excel escape hatch” mapping was the most valuable research output. It turned a vague complaint (“sellers export too much”) into a prioritised list of missing decision cues that directly informed the design roadmap.

Why This Matters

This wasn’t a dashboard redesign

This work didn’t just improve a dashboard. It changed how enterprise sellers decide what to do next — with greater confidence, clarity, and speed.

The shift from reporting to decision-enablement is the kind of design intervention that doesn’t show up in screenshots but shows up in adoption, trust, and operational efficiency. It required:

  • System-level thinking — seeing beyond individual widgets to the decision architecture
  • Designing for trust — treating data confidence as a first-class UX requirement
  • Research-driven iteration — using seller behaviour (not stakeholder opinions) as the design compass
  • AI as a multiplier — compressing synthesis and ideation without replacing judgement
Design Impact

Commerce Insights demonstrates that the most impactful design work often isn’t visual — it’s structural. Changing what a product is (a decision system vs. a dashboard) matters more than changing how it looks.
