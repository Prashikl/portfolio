Reframing a dashboard into a decision system.

Sellers had rich data and poor decisions. Every quarter, the same exports landed in the same spreadsheets, and the same questions still got answered too late. I led a structural reframe — from reporting surface into a system that tells a seller what to do next, and why.

i
The context

Rich data, poor decisions

The original surface was a reporting dashboard. Numbers, charts, filters. It told sellers what was happening. It rarely told them what to do — and never told them what to do first.

I shadowed sellers and CSMs across three regions. The pattern repeated: open dashboard, scan, export, paste into Excel, build a side analysis, share that side analysis with the team. The dashboard was a passing-through surface, not a decision surface.

What sellers had

A dashboard of what is. Filterable, exportable, faithful to the data warehouse. Slow to translate into action.

What sellers needed

A surface of what next. Ranked, framed as actions, with the trust signals to defend the choice in a QBR.

ii
The reframe

Three principles, written in the surface

The reframe wasn't a redesign. It was a re-statement of what the surface is for. I authored three principles that every widget, label, and number had to honour. If a widget couldn't, it didn't ship.

i.

Decision over reporting

Every widget had to answer one question: what should I do next? If a widget showed a number without a follow-on action, it wasn't earning its place on the surface.

ii.

Trust as a UX requirement

Data freshness, source context, and confidence cues had to be visible — not buried in tooltips. Sellers were going to defend these numbers in QBRs. The UI had to defend them first.

iii.

Speed to decision

Sellers don't read raw values; they read change. Deltas, risk thresholds, and direction of travel ranked higher than the underlying number. The number was the citation, not the headline.

Reframe

A dashboard tells you what is. A decision system tells you what to do, and gives you the receipts.

iii
The decisions

Four moves that changed what the surface is

01

Action-framed insights

Every insight was authored as a sentence: "As a seller, I can ___ so that ___." If we couldn't write the sentence, we didn't ship the widget. This single discipline killed more dead weight than any review meeting.

02

Widget-based architecture

Replaced the monolithic dashboard with composable, role-aware widgets. A field seller, a CSM, and a leader saw three different surfaces — built from the same set of pieces, ranked by what each role had to decide that week.

03

Trust signals, surfaced

Freshness stamps, source attributions, and confidence cues lived inline with the data — not under an info-icon. Sellers stopped asking "is this right?" and started asking "what do I do about it?"

04

Risk & delta highlighting

Movement was the headline. Raw values were demoted to citations beneath the delta. Risk thresholds were colour-coded against the seller's own targets — not a global benchmark.

iv
Research

The Excel escape hatch

Across every shadow session, sellers exported data into Excel. Always. The instinct was to call this an export problem and add a better export. The instinct was wrong.

Insight

Every Excel export is a missing decision cue. Sellers don't want spreadsheets — they want answers the dashboard didn't give them.

Once the team accepted this framing, the export button stopped being a feature and started being a diagnostic. Each high-volume export pointed to a decision the surface had failed to make legible. We catalogued the top fifteen, and each one became a widget candidate.

AI as a synthesis accelerator

I used AI to cluster and structure workshop transcripts and seller interviews — turning hours of unstructured signal into ranked themes in minutes. The judgement calls — what mattered, what was noise, what would survive a QBR — stayed with me and the research lead.

vi
Why it matters

The most impactful work isn't visual

This wasn't a dashboard redesign. The grids look similar. The colour palette is conservative. If you were judging on screenshots, you'd miss the work.

The real change was structural: a different answer to the question what is this surface for. The visual layer followed. It always does.

Lesson

The most impactful design work often isn't visual — it's structural. A reframe is worth a hundred refinements.
