Origin

The question behind the project

Most people use AI as a chatbot — ask a question, get an answer, hope it’s right. I wanted to test a different hypothesis: can you design an information architecture that makes an AI reliably expert in a specialist domain?

I chose a domain deliberately outside my professional work — one that combines hard functional constraints (ergonomic standards, safety codes, measurable specifications) with subjective creative judgement. If you can structure AI reasoning for that kind of complexity, the pattern transfers to any domain.

This wasn’t a client brief. It was a design exercise in AI systems architecture — treating prompt engineering not as writing instructions, but as designing how an AI should think.

Core Hypothesis

Prompt engineering is an information architecture problem, not a writing problem. If you structure domain knowledge into the right layers — principles, playbooks, prompts, references — the AI becomes reliably expert instead of inconsistently helpful.

How I designed the operating principles

These aren’t suggestions — they’re hard constraints that force the AI to reason like an expert rather than a generalist. Each one encodes a specific expert behaviour:

01
Enforce a reasoning sequence. The AI must work through function → flow → mood → materials → lighting → procurement. No skipping ahead to aesthetics before functional constraints are resolved.
02
Start with movement, not objects. Map circulation paths before placing anything. This mirrors how experts think — space is defined by how people move through it.
03
Layer, don’t flatten. Complex outputs (like lighting) require layered thinking: ambient + task + accent, planned per activity zone. The AI can’t give a single-fixture answer.
04
Distinguish precision from preference. Some values are measurable standards (CRI, CCT, clearance distances). Others are subjective. The AI must know which is which.
05
Validate against published standards. Where industry standards exist, the AI must use them — not approximate. (e.g., NKBA clearances, BIFMA ergonomic ranges.)
06
Apply ergonomic constraints automatically. Workstation setups use published body-to-object distance ranges, not guesswork.
07
Treat environmental factors as behavioural. Lighting, acoustics, and temperature aren’t just comfort — they influence behaviour, productivity, and dwell time.
08
Treat acoustics as structural. Sound absorption, zoning, and privacy are design inputs — not afterthoughts to fix later.

15 domain playbooks, same structure

Each playbook follows an identical 6-output format: define goals & constraints, create zoning maps, generate layout options, build material palettes, plan environmental systems, compile procurement lists. The consistency is deliberate — it’s what makes the system scalable and auditable.

Design Decisions

Four choices that shaped the system

Constraints before creativity

I structured the AI to resolve functional constraints before generating creative options. The reasoning sequence is enforced, not suggested. This prevents the most common AI failure: producing beautiful outputs that violate basic domain rules.

Evidence over opinion

Every operating principle links to a published standard. AI hallucinations become dangerous when someone acts on them in a specialist domain. The reference layer makes every claim auditable and every recommendation traceable.

Combinatorial scale

Instead of writing prompts individually, I designed a system that generates combinations: 15 playbooks × 15 styles × 10 constraints × 6 outputs = thousands of unique, useful prompts. The structure is the product, not the individual prompts.

Domain-agnostic architecture

The 4-layer structure (principles → playbooks → prompts → references) isn’t specific to any domain. It’s a reusable template for making AI reliably expert in any specialist field — from healthcare protocols to financial compliance to engineering standards.

What This Proves

How this connects to my professional work

Prompt engineering is information architecture. I didn’t write prompts — I designed a system for how an AI should reason about a domain. Deciding what it should know, in what order, and what it should never assume. That’s the same skill as designing an enterprise workflow: structuring decisions so users reach the right outcome reliably.

The pattern transfers directly. The principle that makes this promptbook work — structured constraints produce trusted outputs — is the same principle behind my enterprise work at Microsoft. Orders Self-Serve works because I surfaced eligibility upfront. This promptbook works because I enforced constraints-before-creativity. Same architecture, different domain.

Intellectual curiosity as practice. I built this on my own time because I wanted to understand AI tool design at a structural level — not just how to use AI, but how to architect systems that make AI reliable. The best designers I know spend time outside their comfort zone, stress-testing their own frameworks.

Trust requires structure. The same evidence-first philosophy from my enterprise work applies here. Every claim is source-backed. Every principle is grounded in a published standard. Zero unsourced claims. Making AI trustworthy isn’t about better prompts — it’s about better information architecture.
